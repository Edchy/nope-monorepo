## Executive Summary

NOPE's written content — blog posts, essays, notes, project writeups — follows the same operating principle as everything else: say the thing, then stop. Most brand blogs are structured around volume, SEO targets, and imaginary audiences. NOPE doesn't do that. Each post exists because there's something worth saying, not because it's Thursday.

The voice in long-form content is the same as everywhere else: calm, dry, confident, opinionated. But long-form gives more room for argument — and NOPE uses that room to make a real point, not to fill space. A NOPE post reads like it was written by someone who thought carefully, cut liberally, and published when it was ready.

Posts should feel like something a smart, quiet person said at the right moment. Not a lecture. Not a content play. Just the thing, said well.

## What NOPE Writes About

*Confidence: High*

NOPE writes about things it has an actual opinion on. The filter: would the right reader find this useful, surprising, or clarifying? If not, it probably doesn't get written.

**Natural NOPE territory:**
- Design decisions — why something was removed, not added
- Craft and process — what the actual work looks like
- Things that are overrated (with reasoning, not snark)
- Small observations about the built environment: software, interfaces, physical objects
- What "good" looks like in specific, concrete terms
- What NOPE learned from a specific project or mistake

**What NOPE doesn't write about:**
- Trend roundups ("5 design trends for 2026")
- Generic advice ("how to be more creative")
- Anything starting from "what if we wrote about X for SEO"
- Hot takes timed to news cycles
- Posts that exist to exist

## Post Structure

*Confidence: High*

### The Rule

Lead with the point. Use the body to support it. Stop when it's done.

There is no required structure beyond this. No introduction that says what the post will cover. No conclusion that summarizes what was covered. The reader can see both.

### Opening

The first sentence does the work. It states the argument, makes the observation, or drops the reader into the situation. No warm-up, no throat-clearing.

**Good opening:**
> Most interfaces try to solve problems. The good ones try to disappear.

**Bad opening:**
> In this post, we want to share some thoughts on interface design and what we've learned about what makes a great user experience.

The bad one announces itself. The good one just starts.

### Body

Short paragraphs. Rarely more than 3–4 sentences. Single-sentence paragraphs are fine when they land something.

Use white space. Long blocks of text are a design failure.

Make arguments, not observations. "Many designers do X" is an observation. "X is wrong because Y" is an argument. NOPE writes arguments.

One idea per paragraph. When it's done, break.

### Ending

The last sentence is the last thing the reader takes with them. Make it count.

Don't write a conclusion. Don't summarize. Don't end with a call to action dressed up as reflection.

A NOPE post ends when the argument is complete. Sometimes that's a single crisp line that closes the idea. Sometimes it just stops. Both are fine.

**Good ending:**
> That's the job. Remove things until what's left works.

**Bad ending:**
> We hope this has given you some food for thought. What do you think? Let us know in the comments below, and don't forget to follow us for more insights.

## Headline Writing

*Confidence: High*

### Principles

Declarative, not clickbait. The headline states what the post is about or what it argues. It does not tease, promise, or create false urgency.

No question marks. Questions as headlines are almost always a hedge — a way to avoid committing to the point. NOPE commits.

No numbers in headlines unless the number is the point. "7 ways to improve your design" is padding dressed as structure. "One decision that changed the project" is a different thing.

Short enough to scan. Long enough to be specific.

### Examples

| Good | Bad |
|------|-----|
| "Remove it" | "You Won't Believe How Much Removing Features Can Help" |
| "Why we rebuilt it without a framework" | "Is Framework-Free Development Right For You?" |
| "The client said no. They were right." | "5 Things We Learned From Client Feedback" |
| "Good coffee doesn't announce itself" | "What Coffee Can Teach Us About Brand Voice" |
| "Less is not the goal. Better is." | "How Minimalism Changed Our Design Process" |

## Tone-by-Context Matrix

*Confidence: Medium — inferred from brand principles; no NOPE editorial content yet published*

| Content Type | Formality | Energy | Technical Depth | Key Principle |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Blog post / essay | Low | Low–Medium | Varies | Lead with the point. Argue it. Stop. |
| Project writeup | Low | Low | Medium–High | What happened, what was decided, what was learned |
| Short note / observation | Very Low | Low | Low | One thing. Said well. That's it. |
| Newsletter | Low | Low–Medium | Low | Like a letter, not a broadcast |
| About / bio | Very Low | Low | None | Plainly stated. No performance. |

### Blog Posts
- **Overall tone**: Opinionated and calm. You have a view; you're not shouting it.
- **Opening approach**: First sentence is the argument or the scene. No setup.
- **Do's**: Short paragraphs. Specific examples. Sentences that earn their place.
- **Don'ts**: Summaries, intros, conclusions, calls to action that beg. No exclamation marks.
- **Length**: As long as it needs to be. No cap.

### Project Writeups
- **Overall tone**: Honest and specific. What actually happened, not the PR version.
- **Opening approach**: What the project was. One sentence.
- **Do's**: Show decisions. Show what was removed. Show what didn't work.
- **Don'ts**: Vague praise ("we're proud of how this turned out"). Avoid making it a case study with bullet points.
- **Example**: "The client wanted five features. We built two. The product is better for it."

### Newsletter
- **Overall tone**: Personal but not intimate. Like a letter to someone you respect.
- **Opening approach**: Get to the thing. Don't announce what's in the issue.
- **Do's**: One main idea per issue. A sentence or two on what's been built or published.
- **Don'ts**: "This week on the NOPE blog…" — just link it. No roundup energy.

## Writing Rules

*Confidence: High*

These are the mechanical rules. They apply to everything NOPE publishes.

**Punctuation**
- No exclamation marks. Ever.
- No em dashes. Ever. If a sentence needs an em dash, it needs rewriting. Break it into two sentences, or cut the aside entirely.
- Ellipses signal trailing off. Usually better to just end the sentence.

**Sentence structure**
- Short sentences are the default. Long ones should earn it.
- Active voice. "We removed it" not "It was removed."
- Cut every sentence that repeats something already said.

**Words**
- Use the simplest word that works. Not the one that sounds smart. "Remove" not "eliminate." "Use" not "leverage." "Hard" not "challenging."
- If a topic is complex, explain it like you would to someone who has never heard of it. Simple words. Short sentences. Even childish words if they make the idea land. The goal is understanding, not sounding clever.
- Avoid jargon. If you use a term the reader might not know, explain it in plain language in the next sentence. Or cut it and use a simpler word.
- Avoid: innovative, seamless, curated, optimised, tailored, powerful, robust, cutting-edge.
- Never: "we're excited to share," "it goes without saying," "at the end of the day."

**Formatting**
- Subheadings only when the post is long enough to need navigation. Don't impose structure on a short piece.
- Bullet points only when items are genuinely list-like. Not as a way to break up paragraphs.
- Bold only when a word genuinely needs emphasis. Not for decoration.

## Terminology Guide

Inherits from the main brand voice guidelines. Additions specific to editorial content:

### Must-Use in Content
| Term | Usage | Instead Of |
|------|-------|------------|
| Remove / removed | For editing, cutting, simplifying | "streamline", "pare back" |
| Signal | For what matters, what's useful | "value", "takeaway" |
| Useful | For functional quality | "impactful", "actionable" |
| Made | For things created by people | "generated", "produced" |

### Never Use in Content
| Term | Reason |
|------|--------|
| "Deep dive" | Overused. Just write the thing. |
| "Unpack" | Same problem. |
| "Learnings" | Use "what we learned." |
| "Takeaway" | Reduce, don't abstract. |
| "Content" to describe NOPE's writing | It's writing, not content. |
| "Space" (as in "the design space") | Jargon. Say what you mean. |
| Em dash (—) | Banned. Rewrite the sentence. Two shorter sentences beat one long one with a detour in the middle. |
| Unexplained jargon | If a reader has to google a word to follow the argument, the word is wrong. Explain it or replace it. |

## Language That Works

*Confidence: Medium — inferred from brand identity; will strengthen as real posts are written*

### Phrases That Fit NOPE Writing
1. **"That's it."** — Ending a paragraph that completes a thought. Signals: we're done here.
2. **"We removed it."** — When explaining a design or editing decision. Frames absence as action.
3. **"Probably not."** — Honest answer to a question the post is implicitly asking.
4. **"It works."** — Two words. Confident. Done.
5. **"That'll do."** — Understated approval. The highest NOPE compliment.

### Openings That Work
- Start with a specific observation: "Most [thing] do [problem]."
- Start with a decision: "We rebuilt it without [X]."
- Start with a contradiction: "The brief was simple. The answer wasn't."
- Start with a plain fact that carries weight: "We said no fourteen times. The client was right once."

## Content Examples

### On-brand post opening

> **Remove it**
>
> The easiest version of a feature is the one you don't build.
>
> We spent three weeks designing an onboarding flow. Tested it. Iterated. The drop-off was still 40%.
>
> So we removed it.
>
> Signup went up. Support tickets went down. Nobody asked where the onboarding went.

*Why it works*: Opens with the point. Short paragraphs. The story is specific. The ending doesn't explain itself.

### Off-brand post opening

> **5 Ways Restraint Can Transform Your Design Process**
>
> In today's fast-paced digital landscape, designers are constantly challenged to do more with less. But what if the key to creating truly impactful work wasn't about adding — it was about removing? In this post, we'll explore five powerful strategies for embracing restraint in your design practice.

*What's wrong*: Numbered list in the headline. "Fast-paced digital landscape" is zombie language. The opening announces itself instead of starting. "Powerful strategies" is exactly the kind of phrase NOPE is built against.
